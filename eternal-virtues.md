## Satyam · Truth · સત્યમ્

### "God" — the first thought
Weeks after his 1998 heart bypass, a Pulitzer Prize-winning editor asked Swamishri what first crossed his mind when he woke from surgery. Without hesitation: "God." Surprised, the journalist pressed — wasn't he disappointed to be back on earth, and not in God's presence? "No. God is always with us." The interviewer, who had grilled politicians and spiritual leaders alike, told the assembly afterward that these answers had no hidden agenda — only sincerity. He left convinced there was no performance in this sadhu, only truth.

### "Have you ever seen God?"
In Rajkot in 1991, an unfamiliar youth stopped Swamishri on his way back from the evening assembly with the boldest question there is: "Have you ever seen God?" Swamishri stopped and smiled. "I have. Hence I can experience his bliss and speak of him." "So, you know God?" the youth pressed. "Yes, I know God." No lecture, no defensiveness — just the calm of someone describing an old friend. The youth needed nothing more.

### The stillness in the noise
A young devotee from Toronto watched Swamishri sit for his morning puja amid a din of arriving crowds, conversations, and bhajans with instruments. To his astonishment, Swamishri calmly applied the tilak-chandlo, closed his eyes, and entered deep meditation — serene, as if no one were around him at all. Just the two of them: him and God. The youth later said that single sight of truthful devotion changed him forever.

### The skeptic at the Bhavan
Shri Manek Dalal, chairman of the Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan in London, avoided sadhus — he believed they misused spiritual authority for personal gain. One day he happened to walk past a small gathering where Swamishri was speaking and decided to sit for a few minutes. The unforced sincerity of what he heard undid years of bias. "I have heard many sadhus and mahatmas speak," he admitted, "but none so genuine and simple. There is no manipulation in his speech."

### "This is who I am"
When Swamishri became guru after Yogiji Maharaj's passing, an attendant suggested he adopt his beloved predecessor's style — clap during bhajans, give dhabbaas, sway — so devotees would love him the same way. Swamishri refused gently: "That was Yogiji Maharaj's way of doing things. It doesn't make sense for me to copy him. This is who I am. I am not good at impersonating other people." Truth begins with refusing to be an imitation.

### "I only stock real things"
After an exhausting day in which Swamishri blessed five thousand devotees one by one, a sadhu joked that they should fabricate a motorized wooden hand to do the blessing — no one would be able to tell. Swamishri interrupted before the joke could finish: "Why would you want to deceive them? I don't keep fake things. I only stock real things." Even in jest, he would not let falsehood come near his devotees.

### A life with no secrets
During a meeting in Gondal, a sadhu noticed Swamishri scribbling dates on a small notepad and teased: "What are you writing in that private diary?" Swamishri replied, "My whole life is open to the public. There are no secrets. If you have secrets, you always fear them being revealed. I live my life openly, with God as my witness." Eating, sleeping, reading, writing — not a single moment of his life is hidden from anyone.

### Fifty years, one set of principles
Mahant Swami, who witnessed half a century of BAPS's growth, observed that political and spiritual leaders change their principles every year — sometimes every six months. Pramukh Swami Maharaj led the Sanstha for over fifty years, and his ideals never moved. Like Bhagwan Ramchandra, of whom Valmiki wrote that his word and action were always one, Swamishri never needed double standards. That consistency, Mahant Swami said, is the true measure of his truthfulness.

### "The truth can be bitter"
Blessing the Inspector General of Police of Bihar in 2006, Swamishri gave counsel that could guide any officer, parent, or child: "God is pleased with those who side with the truth. We don't bear ill-will toward anybody, but the truth must be established. The truth can be bitter, but in the long run it is always beneficial. You may have to endure at first — but the truth always prevails."

### "It will rain"
Weeks before the 1985 bicentenary celebrations of Gunatitanand Swami, Gujarat stood on the edge of famine — three months into monsoon season without a cloud in the sky. Community leaders begged to postpone the festival. Swamishri asked everyone to pray, and said simply: "The festival will be celebrated as planned. It will rain. God is merciful; he will listen to our prayers." To everyone's astonishment the rains came, the famine fears dissolved, and the festival went ahead.

### Foundations for a thousand years
When land was acquired for Swaminarayan Akshardham in New Delhi, Swamishri declared the foundations should stand untroubled for a thousand years. India's most renowned structural engineers doubted it was even possible — sandy soil, an earthquake zone, the banks of a river. Swamishri insisted it would be done. Today the monument glistens on the Yamuna, and those same engineers call it a miracle they still cannot fully explain.

### Snow on demand
In March 2003, a film crew sat defeated in Nepal — days of rain, no sun, and no snow on the peaks at Muktinath, where locals insisted there never would be any that season. The organizers phoned Swamishri in Gondal. His answer: "Do the shoot tomorrow. It will be alright." At dawn the mountains stood white under a brilliant sun, and the crew captured an hour of footage in a single day. The award-winning cinematographer called it BAPS's seventeenth department: "Nature. Managed by Pramukh Swami Maharaj's prayers."

### What he told the Pope's press
The day before meeting the Pope in 1985, a Reuters journalist asked Swamishri whether they would discuss politics, as visiting leaders often did. "We do not want to talk about politics," Swamishri replied, "but about how the world can become nobler and happier, and how God can be worshipped by all." Years later in the Czech Republic and at the Wailing Wall in Jerusalem, tourists slowed to watch a Hindu sadhu quietly praying for the people of a land not his own.

### "I will give you God"
In 1988, leaving a devotee's home in Paris, Swamishri was moved by the family's wholehearted service. "You have all served wholeheartedly. What else can I give you in return?" he said. "I have God, so I will give him to you." Ten years later, the renowned surgeon who performed his bypass — a self-proclaimed atheist — prostrated before him on a public stage and said that each day he had visited Swamishri after the operation, it felt like going for the darshan of God.

## Shaucham · Purity · શૌચમ્

### The president cleans the toilets
After a festival in Sarangpur in the 1960s, while the exhausted volunteers napped, a sadhu noticed Swamishri missing — and found the President of BAPS quietly cleaning the overflowing common toilets, carrying buckets to the dump-site and sweeping between rounds. Caught in the act, Swamishri whispered: "Don't make noise — you'll wake the sadhus who are resting. Now, two is better than one. If you want me to finish sooner, get me some water."

### The locked bathroom
Passing through the mandir grounds in Bochasan, Swamishri caught the stench of a bathroom that had gone uncleaned for days. He stepped inside, locked the door from within, and scrubbed it spotless himself. The sadhus and devotees outside assumed he was simply using it. Only when he emerged did they realize what the guru of millions had been doing behind that locked door.

### Leave it better than you found it
Staying at a devotee's newly built house in Anand, Swamishri began scrubbing the paint and cement stains off the new bathroom tiles. "We're only here for a day and a half — why bother?" a sadhu protested. Swamishri kept cleaning: "We should leave the place in better condition than we received it. The devotee was kind enough to let us stay. They might not have had time to clean up. We should help out — it is our seva."

### "So that God would like to stay there"
Swamishri has visited over 250,000 homes, and each padhramani carries the same gentle message: you do not need a large home, but a tidy one. "Our home and mandir should be clean, so that God would like to stay there," he says. "How can you expect God to stay where there is filth? If everyone kept their home and neighbourhood clean, the whole country would be a cleaner place." Kitchen things belong in the kitchen — and children learn what they see.

### The air-conditioner sermon
In Atladra, Swamishri stopped beside youths cleaning the dust-clogged filters of an old A.C. unit and turned the chore into a teaching: "The filters filled with dirt, so they had to be removed and cleaned. In the same way, when life becomes spoilt by bad habits, it is degraded. Inspect your character for impurities — and cleanse it." A machine part became a mirror.

### The doctor who cleans toilets
Dr. Rajiv Vyas of New Jersey leads a staff of over 38 physicians and 800 healthcare employees. Yet every morning before work he goes to the mandir — and cleans its public toilets. In Surat, affluent youths do the same every Sunday; in London, doctors have washed the mandir's dishes every Saturday for over two decades. To all of them Swamishri has given the same blessing: "You have worked to purify the outside; God will purify your hearts and souls."

### The impromptu inspections
To this day Swamishri inspects every corner of even the smallest mandirs — kitchens, dairy rooms, storage areas, public toilets and the garbhagruha. At the student hostel in Vidyanagar his unannounced room checks send students scrambling; his trained eye sorts the truly clean from the hastily hidden, sweetly reprimanding one and praising the other. In Sarangpur in 1997 he entered a bathroom and immediately summoned the housekeeping team — to bless them: "This is very clean. It should be like this all the time, and everywhere."

### Purity in business
Meeting a devotee and his partner in New York in 2004, the conversation turned to their dairy business. Swamishri's advice was practical and pointed: "Whenever you make yogurt or paneer, or even process milk, hygiene must be the priority. Scrub every utensil and machine before you produce anything. You come from a family that is very particular about its purity — it only makes sense for you to do this." For Swamishri, cleanliness is devotion carried into the workplace.

### The tribal devotee's testimony
A tribal devotee from the village of Uber once described his transformation: after Swamishri's darshan and blessings, his entire family gave up meat, alcohol, and smoking, and their violent tempers cooled. "It has been eight months and I can say we feel one hundred percent purified," he said. "Even my adulterous behaviour is gone." Only one who is himself clean, pure and spotless can transform the lives of others this way.

## Daya · Compassion · દયા

### The 2 a.m. prayers
In 1990 the monsoon failed Gujarat while Swamishri was thousands of miles away in London. His response wasn't a statement — it was lost sleep. Night after night his attendant found him awake at 2 a.m., sitting up in bed, praying to Bhagwan Swaminarayan for rain on soil an ocean away. From then on, the attendant quietly joined the nightly prayers. In a letter home Swamishri wrote: "It is natural that you are worried, but have faith in God. I am praying daily. There is still time."

### The hungry calves
At a government cattle camp near Rajkot during the 1987 famine, a group of starving calves crowded around Swamishri and followed him as he walked. A volunteer explained they hadn't eaten in three days. Swamishri's eyes filled with tears and he left in silence — but the moment he reached Gondal he summoned a sadhu: "The way those calves ran after us was unbearable for me. Arrange to send trucks full of fodder right away." For days afterward he would drift out of conversations, thinking only of the drought.

### Saving the farmers' cattle
Watching their livestock perish in the drought, desperate farmers began selling their cattle. Swamishri stopped them — and organized cattle camps that fed and sheltered the animals for up to a year, until their owners could provide again. He understood the cattle were not just animals but entire livelihoods. One farmer wept seeing his animals so well cared for; Kasambhai, a Muslim resident of Madhupur Gir, said simply: "I feel that Pramukh Swami Maharaj is my Khuda."

### Meals by lunchtime
At 8:46 a.m. on 26 January 2001, a massive earthquake flattened parts of Gujarat. By lunchtime the same day, Swamishri had warm meals moving toward survivors. He mobilized sadhus and volunteers for emergency relief, worked the phones past midnight for a year, and was coordinating arrangements the next morning before he had even showered. More than 500 villages received aid; fifteen were rebuilt entirely — today counted among the finest in Gujarat.

### Two green chillies
Inspecting the relief kitchen where food packets were being prepared for earthquake survivors, Swamishri added an instruction that stunned the volunteers with its tenderness: "Include two green chillies in each packet. People from the Kutch region enjoy spicy food — and they will taste good with the sweets." He had also phoned the mandir grinding the relief grain: only the best quality, cleaned and sifted for small stones. Compassion, down to the smallest detail.

### "Do not harbour any partiality"
Phoning the volunteers who served in the aftermath of the Surat floods, Swamishri gave instructions that define seva: "You have put up with difficulties to help those in need — Bhagwan Swaminarayan will be pleased. Remember, this service is not for recognition. Serve with care so the supplies actually reach the people. And do not harbour any partiality when distributing them. Give to everyone. Keep everyone happy."

### "Not one happy letter"
In London one night, Swamishri finished meeting devotees at 10 p.m. and then sat reading a long letter. When he finished, he sighed: "There is not one happy letter. Only letters describing pain and misery. Hearing about them causes much pain." Many attendants have witnessed it — his eyes welling up at the mere description of a stranger's suffering, as if the pain were his own.

### A 20-page letter
When Harshad Chavda, a youth who had served BAPS for years, was badly injured in a car accident, Swamishri wrote him a letter twenty pages long. "The news brings me much pain. I am praying for you in my morning puja. I should be serving you in your time of need, because you have always served selflessly, without concern for your health, hunger, thirst or time of day. The fact that I cannot serve you now causes me immense sorrow." The guru, apologizing that he could not nurse his disciple.

### The midnight blanket
An elderly devotee arrived in Atladra for a festival at 2 a.m. and, finding everyone asleep, lay down by the assembly-hall steps with his bag for a pillow. He woke at 5:30 to find a blanket wrapped around him. "Who covered me?" he asked the attendant. The answer: "Pramukh Swami Maharaj came in the middle of the night and covered you with his blanket."

### The fever and the thin cloth
On a bitterly cold night in 1977, a sadhu searched everywhere for a spare blanket for a young devotee shivering with fever — it was midnight, and there were none. Moments later Swamishri walked into the room, handed the boy a blanket, and left without a word. Only at dawn did everyone realize it was Swamishri's own; he had passed the freezing night covered by nothing but his thin upper garment.

### Sandals in the scorching sun
During a procession through Nadiad on a blistering 1971 afternoon, Swamishri noticed from his decorated chariot that a sadhu was walking barefoot on the baking pavement. He called him over: "Take these sandals and wear them. The sizzling pavement may damage your health." Realizing they were probably Swamishri's own, the sadhu slipped them off — and Swamishri caught that too: "I didn't give you the sandals to hold in your hands. Wear them!"

### Over the ditches, at eighty
Hearing that a young boy from a small village lay unconscious in a Surat ICU after a festival accident, 80-year-old Swamishri set out for the hospital. The road was closed for construction, so he stepped out of the car and walked the last quarter kilometre over pipes and ditches. At the bedside he placed his hand on the boy's head and whispered, "Prashant… Jai Swaminarayan…", garlanded him, prayed, and comforted the boy's father before trekking back the way he came.

### Eight years for Atul
Walking to an assembly in Ahmedabad, Swamishri spotted a familiar face bow at his feet. A sadhu began the introduction; Swamishri interrupted: "I know him. How are you, Atul? Are you still on it?" — "On what?" — "Brown sugar, Atul. I haven't forgotten." He then gently dismantled every excuse, including the government job that funded the habit: "Your drug habit is one type of stealing, and that is a sin. Then you steal again from people to support it — that is another sin." For eight years Swamishri pursued this one soul, the only son of a widowed mother, until the addiction finally let go.

### "There's nothing you can't live without"
A young hunter promised Swamishri he would give up killing — then murmured under his breath that he couldn't live without hunting. Swamishri heard, looked him in the eye, and said: "There is nothing you can't live without. The poor animals live on grass — do they harm anyone? If somebody hits us, do we not feel pain? They have souls too. You are a Kshatriya; your duty is to protect." The pledge, this time, was real.

### What he prays for
A sadhu once asked Swamishri what he actually thinks of when he prays before Harikrishna Maharaj. The answer was a window into his heart: "What else would we pray for? May everyone be blessed with peace and happiness. May no one have to suffer any miseries. May everyone offer devotion, and their circumstances improve." His life's motto follows: in the joy of others lies our own.

## Kshantihi · Forgiveness · ક્ષાન્તિ:

### The poison kept secret
In 1954, a jealous elder tried to poison the 32-year-old Swamishri — the plot failed at his very lips. Swamishri forgave him unconditionally, made the two witnesses promise never to speak of it, and personally cared for the man in Satsang until the day he died. The story surfaced only six years after the perpetrator's death. When it was finally narrated in a 1989 assembly, Swamishri's eyes filled with tears: "Forget all that. Speak only of the service he performed."

### Lunch for the arresting officer
In 1977, conspirators bribed a police inspector to arrest Swamishri on false charges. The plot collapsed when devotees demanded the nonexistent warrant, and the exposed inspector paced back and forth, trembling, near tears. Swamishri placed a hand on his head: "Don't worry. It is almost lunchtime — take lunch before you leave." Then he had the inspector seated before him and lovingly served him, as if he were an invited guest. The man remembers that mercy to this day.

### Escorting his accusers to safety
In 1966, a misinformed group demanded a closed-door meeting in Ahmedabad and began shouting insults at Swamishri. When outraged youths burst in to defend him, Swamishri pushed them out instead: "There is no need for you to do anything. They are here to talk to me — let them finish." When the meeting broke off, he personally escorted his accusers to the main gate, to make sure no one blocked their way home.

### "You have done all that service to please God"
A devotee returned from America fuming: a jealous group was blocking Swamishri's visit to his town — the same people whose mandir he had helped build. "I am not going to give them a single penny more," he vowed. Swamishri smiled and soothed him: "Do not think like that. You did all that service to please God, not others. Whether or not they invite us — do not worry about it."

### "It never happened"
Historian Makrand Mehta had publicly defamed the Sampradaya and, misreading BAPS's silence, nursed a grudge against Swamishri himself. Years later — humbled by the unconditional hospitality of a devotee host in London, and by a stranger returning his stolen passport in Rome the morning after a desperate prayer — he came to apologize, weeping at Swamishri's feet. Swamishri handed him a glass of water, wiped his tears, and said five words: "Forget everything. It never happened."

### Laughing at slander
Days after the landmark Cultural Festival of India in New York, a sadhu walked in with a pamphlet full of slander against Swamishri and the Sanstha. Swamishri insisted it be read aloud — and at its outrageous title, he doubled up with laughter. When sadhus urged a stern rebuttal, he refused: "If they insult us, curse us, or even hit us, we should never retaliate. If you have extra time, pray to God and do bhajan." He never even asked how many pamphlets had been printed.

### A seat on the sofa
A prominent columnist spent months publishing false accounts about Swamishri and his work. When the sadhus could bear it no longer and prepared responses, Swamishri extracted a promise that they never would: "Shri Krishna was God — he could act as he wished. We are God's servants." Then, meeting the very same writer at a public event, Swamishri invited him up to sit beside him on the sofa, leaving the audience astonished.

### "God does not look at others' faults"
When sadhus distributing famine relief in Bhadra wrote that villagers were sarcastically accusing them of keeping supplies for themselves, Swamishri replied at once: "People will always talk. If you listen to such things you will not be able to do any work. God and his holy Sadhu do not look at others' faults — if God looked at ours, we would never be liberated. Shriji Maharaj always looked for the good in all. We should do the same."

### The widow who forgave
When a village dispute in Rohishala ended with a youth murdering a respected devotee, the town teetered on the edge of vengeance. Swamishri called the victim's son: "Your father is in Akshardham and I am here for you. Withdraw the case. Forgive that ignorant youth — and tell your mother to forgive him too." She did, before a packed courtroom in Bhavnagar, and Swamishri spent two days in the village calming both sides. A villager said it plainly: without him, corpses would have filled the streets.

### Water after 150 years
The villages of Kukad and Odarka had fed a blood feud for a century and a half — 45 villages drawn in, and the British Empire, the King of Bhavnagar, and the Gujarat government had all given up. On 12 April 1990, after years of patient groundwork, Swamishri gathered both clans under one roof for the first time in 150 years and had them drink water from each other's villages. The leaders' verdict: "What the British and Indian governments could not accomplish has been done by Pramukh Swami Maharaj."

### Sweets for two clans
In Gondal in 1997, Swamishri sat from lunch until 4 p.m. mediating between Kshatriya and Patel clans whose three-year feud had already turned bloody. As everyone rose to leave, he called them back for one more thing: "To forgive is priceless. Revenge fuels further revenge. One has already died and others may follow unless you forgive and forget." Then he asked for sweets and fed both clans with his own hands — teaching love by example.

### "Think of it as God's wish"
In Gadhada in 1995, a Kshatriya devotee told Swamishri he intended to avenge his nephew's murder — the village was calling him a coward. Swamishri talked him down from the edge: "Killing him will not bring your nephew back; it will only make you accountable for a life. The people instigating you will not feed your family afterward. Stay calm, quietly pray to God, and think of this as an opportunity for peace." The tension dissolved.

## Tyagaha · Renunciation · ત્યાગ:

### One letter was enough
Eighteen-year-old Shantilal dreamed of higher studies in English. Then a short letter arrived from his guru, Shastriji Maharaj, calling him to renounce. Where sages and kings of old took months or years of deliberation, his decision was instantaneous — he shed worldly life, the book says, as easily as a snake sheds its skin. On 11 November 1939 he was initiated as a parshad, and weeks later became Sadhu Narayanswarupdas — a name the world would come to know as Pramukh Swami Maharaj.

### The reluctant president
At 28, Swamishri refused the presidency of BAPS — he wanted only to serve, never to lead. After weeks of his guru's loving insistence he finally accepted, and before the gathered devotees in 1950 he made a vow: "I will serve the Sanstha loyally and faithfully, without concern for my body, and work my hardest to repay the trust you have placed in me." He has kept that promise every single day since.

### Not one square inch
Under Swamishri's hand, BAPS grew from humble origins into a worldwide organization — hundreds of mandirs, monuments, hospitals and schools. Yet not a single square inch of land, not one penny, stands in his name. Every donation flows onward to serve humanity. The man who built it all owns nothing of it.

### Serving without the fruit
The Gita calls him the true renunciant who gives up not good deeds, but the desire for their fruits. Swamishri puts it simply: "Serve with the intent of pleasing God. Never worry about the result — that is God's wish. The only expectation is that God is pleased; apart from that, never wish for anything in return." It may be easy to leave home and possessions — but to renounce credit is nearly impossible. He has done it for seventy years.

### Advice to a restless scholar
A scholar of the Upanishads and the Gita confessed to Swamishri in Mumbai: "Even though I have studied the scriptures, I feel lost and uneasy." Swamishri answered from lived experience: "We carry the weight of our actions. Offer everything to God and it causes no attachment. The feeling that 'I did this, so I deserve the benefit' nourishes the ego — and that is why there is conflict. Leave everything to God."

### "Why take the burden?"
At 10:30 p.m. after a long, taxing administrative meeting, Swamishri's physician began to ask whether he felt stressed. Swamishri laughed before the question was even finished, folded his hands and said: "Jai Swaminarayan. Not at all. Why take the burden for it all? I always go to sleep believing God is the all-doer. Whatever has happened, and whatever will happen, is by his wish."

### "Not capable of breaking a dry leaf"
A sadhu asked Swamishri how it feels to be honoured and respected for all he has accomplished. "The honour is for God," Swamishri replied. "I am not capable of breaking even a dry leaf. It is because we follow God's wish and please him that we are respected — no one would even look at us otherwise. Serve humbly, without expectations."

## Arjavam · Simplicity · આર્જવમ્

### The van in the mud
On the way to a village ceremony in 1975, the van sank into a rain-soaked dirt road. As sadhus gathered stones for the wheels, Swamishri — soaked through — joined in carrying them, waving off protests: "I used to lift stones in Shastriji Maharaj's time. It's nothing new." When the van still wouldn't budge, he said, "Let's start walking." They finished the journey by foot and bullock cart in the drenching rain, Swamishri singing the Swaminarayan dhun the whole way.

### "Our body is made of dust as well"
In Karel in 1985, an attendant began dusting off Swamishri's dirt-covered blanket before bed. Swamishri asked what he was doing, then remarked: "Our body is made of dust as well." That cold night he slept under the light blanket without complaint — and in the morning the sadhus found he had, in his sleep, wrapped a mosquito net around himself for warmth.

### The president sleeps on the floor
Arriving exhausted at the Vidyanagar hostel past 1 a.m. during summer vacation, Swamishri knocked on the one door with lights still on — students reading late. When one offered to wake the parshads for proper arrangements, Swamishri stopped him: "Let them sleep. We will just sleep here on the floor." And so the president of the Sanstha slept on the floor while the students slept in their beds.

### "I am attracted only to God"
In London in 2004, a magazine editor asked the world traveller a natural question: "You travel around the world — which city or place attracts you most?" Swamishri's answer needed only five words: "I am attracted only to God." More than 15,500 city, town and village visits, and his attendants confirm he has never once asked for a particular car, bed, room, or comfort.

### Five padhramanis became 102
On a festival day in 1983, after a procession in the scorching heat and an assembly, a devotee asked at 6 p.m. whether Swamishri might do "four or five" home visits. Swamishri immediately agreed — and the five became one hundred and two. He sat down to dinner at 10:30 that night, having sanctified more homes in one evening than most would in a year, without one word of complaint.

### "Yogiji Maharaj travelled to all these villages"
Climbing the steep mounds of Bamangam at 3 p.m., Mahant Swami was near collapse and burning with pity — why was Swamishri, at his age, enduring this? Just as he was about to insist on rest, Swamishri smiled and spoke first: "Yogiji Maharaj had travelled to all of these villages." He wasn't thinking about his aching feet at all — only about walking in his guru's footsteps.

### Summer, for the farmers' sake
A sadhu suggested the obvious: tour the blistering villages after monsoon, and spend summers in milder Mumbai. Swamishri refused — "After the monsoon the villagers are busy with their farms. It would inconvenience them." He then toured 87 villages in 27 days in heat that reached 45 degrees, so that the farmers wouldn't have to choose between their guru and their harvest.

### The waste cart up the slope
Late one night in 1962, after festival crowds had gone home, a lone youth strained to push a heavy cart of waste up a slope to the dumpster — and found no one to help. Then Swamishri, just out of a meeting, ran over, put his shoulder to the cart, helped empty the garbage, and stayed until the cart was returned. That youth became a sadhu, and tells the story still.

### Frying puris on an oil can
The day before he was to consecrate the Kolkata mandir in 1972, Swamishri passed the kitchen and saw a sadhu making puris with no one to fry them. He flipped an empty oil can over, sat on it, and started frying. Years earlier in Rajkot, sadhus who arrived late found Yogiji Maharaj already eating — "Pramukh Swami cooked while I did katha," he beamed. "Taste it! You will lick your fingers clean."

### "It's a good thing he ended the speech"
Mid-discourse in a village square, a well-meaning devotee — thinking the timing wrong — abruptly called out the jay nad and ended Swamishri's speech. Swamishri simply smiled, stopped talking, and went to lunch. Asked later about the rudeness, he said: "It's a good thing he ended it. I didn't know there was another assembly in the evening." No embarrassment, no wounded pride — nothing to wound.

### Literally two words
Around 11 p.m. in Nairobi, a senior devotee announced to the waiting crowd that Swamishri would say "two words" — the customary phrase for a short blessing. Swamishri followed the cue precisely and said exactly two words: "Brahma-satyam, jagan-mithya" — Brahman is the eternal Truth; this world is perishable. A complete philosophy, delivered with a twinkle, in two words.

### Laughing at his own Hindi
Addressing thousands alongside two visiting Hindi scholars in 2004, Swamishri switched from Gujarati to Hindi — and produced such a comical blend of both that the whole assembly began to laugh. Swamishri laughed hardest of all: "This is how I speak Hindi — it's not that good, so don't laugh!" Another time, told at dinner that he had mis-cited a reference seven or eight times while giving blessings, he chuckled: "Yes, I do get things mixed up. Scholars worry about reputation. For me it doesn't matter — as long as we speak to please God."

### Reading letters until the microphone clicked on
At his 70th birthday celebrations, while leaders and sadhus praised him before thousands, the renowned photographer Raghu Rai watched Swamishri never once look up — he kept reading and answering devotees' letters. "He was writing right up to the point the microphone was put in front of him and switched on," Rai marveled. "He started speaking at a moment's notice. He was not even thinking about it."

### The leaky apartment
In 1986 Swamishri stayed in a medical student's apartment where rainwater dripped from the ceilings. Twenty years later the devotee, now a doctor, apologized for the inconvenience his guru had endured. Swamishri put him at ease: "One should learn to deal with happiness and sorrow. That kind of experience trains you for tough times. One should learn to be happy and comfortable everywhere."

## Santoshaha · Contentment · સંતોષ:

### "Make do with whatever one gets"
Yogiji Maharaj's motto was "kat vadi javu" — adjust instantly, make do with whatever one gets, whenever and wherever. Swamishri has lived it into his late eighties. When organizers once replaced his cherished practice of meeting every devotee individually with a faster four-line darshan system, he argued briefly, then yielded with grace: "It is best to listen to a sadhu. I will do as you say. God resides in all of you."

### "Where there is love, there is no trouble"
A Mumbai host apologized for the inconveniences Swamishri had endured to reach his home. Swamishri interrupted him mid-sentence: "Where there is such love, there is no trouble. One who wants to worship God should ignore inconveniences — make do with whatever one gets. As long as one can worship God, nothing else matters, even if one only has the shade of a tree."

### The formula for peace
Swamishri often distills contentment into a single teaching: "If one learns to be content with what God gives, there is no sorrow. If we wish for things and don't get them, we become distraught. If one is content with whatever God gives, peace is attained." Asked by a London headmaster how to teach the world to live in harmony, his whole answer was one sentence: "If everyone learns to compromise, world peace would become a reality."

## Samyam · Equality · સામ્યમ્

### After President Clinton, the villagers
In 2001, President Bill Clinton altered his India itinerary to meet Swamishri a second time, and praised him at length. The moment the President's car pulled away, while sadhus buzzed about what Clinton had said, Swamishri's eyes were already searching the crowd — for three villagers from earthquake-struck Kutch. He held their hands, reassured them — "You have faith in God, and he will help you through this difficult time" — and instructed the sadhus: "Book them a sleeper coach home. They will get tired in a seating-only coach."

### The same enthusiasm
Minutes after tens of thousands honoured him at his birthday celebrations in Mahesana, Swamishri arrived at a host's house — where a poor villager stood waiting. Swamishri greeted him and fell into warm conversation as if the grand assembly had never happened. A senior sadhu watching from a distance wondered at it: how does a man praised by thousands, minutes later, give a poor farmer the same undivided joy?

### Finding Dhanji
During grand mandir celebrations in New Delhi in 1994, Swamishri asked the sadhus if they had seen Dhanji Vaghri, a humble devotee from Surendranagar living in Delhi. No one even knew him. Unsatisfied with "he probably did darshan from a distance," Swamishri had Surendranagar phoned for the address and sent a man to fetch him. The same week he received Delhi's elite, he would not let one poor devotee go unmet.

### "Hira was one of us"
When Hira Bharvad, an elderly devotee who had tended the Sarangpur livestock for years, passed away, Swamishri personally arranged his memorial rites — instructing that every 12th and 13th-day ritual be completed and that someone accompany his son to scatter the ashes in the sacred rivers. When the family marveled at the care taken for a simple herdsman, Swamishri's answer was five words: "Hira was one of us."

### "What about the drivers?"
Organizers proudly presented their plan to distribute 70,000 prasad packets in fifteen minutes at a festival. Swamishri listened patiently, then asked the question no one had thought of: "People in the assembly will get prasad — but what about those waiting outside? The tractor, bus and car drivers waiting to take everyone home should also get packets. No one should be left out." Whenever dignitaries visit him, it is the driver and the secretary he quietly makes sure are fed.

### "We are also hari na jan"
In Sarangpur, Swamishri noticed a group of youths keeping their distance and called them over. "Swamiji, we are harijans," they explained. Swamishri smiled: "We are also hari na jan — God's children. Come here, do not hesitate." They ran to him and fell at his feet; no one had ever called them with such affection. He asked about their studies, warned them off addictions, and sent them away standing taller.

### Huts like places of pilgrimage
Dalubhai Madari of Dedvasan had given up every addiction after joining satsang, and longed to host Swamishri in his hut — which doubled as his cowshed, with nowhere proper to sit. Swamishri sat on a cleaned patch of stone, helped Dalubhai perform Thakorji's puja, and when he heard that every tribesman present had also given up addictions, rose and embraced them one by one. "They may be poor, but their darshan brings peace," he said. "These huts are like places of pilgrimage."

### Worth thousands of rupees
Walking to the mandir in Sankari, Swamishri stopped beside a group of listless tribal villagers and greeted them until they met his eyes. Then he spoke to them like family: the mandir is for everyone, not the wealthy; give up the addictions that eat the money that could fix your leaking roof and school your children. "We don't want your money," he finished. "But if you come and do darshan every day, it is worth thousands of rupees to us."

### The tradition he changed
By long-standing custom carried into the Sampradaya, parshads from the lowest strata of society were never given bhagvati diksha — initiation into the saffron robes of a sadhu. In 1981, Swamishri quietly set the custom aside and initiated them — likely the first act of its kind in the Hindu community. Equality, for him, was not a speech; it was a decision.

### A hundred Alexandra Palaces
Days after a majestic celebration at London's Alexandra Palace in 1974, Swamishri was staying in a mud house in a nondescript Saurashtra village. A London youth travelling with him remarked, "This is a far cry from Alexandra Palace!" Swamishri didn't hesitate: "Subhash, a hundred Alexandra Palaces do not add up to this little mud house. Look at the affection of these poor devotees."

### The campaign he never answered
A group once launched an all-out media campaign against Swamishri — thousands of pamphlets and books distributed across India, with insults beyond all decency. Swamishri's daily routine did not change; not one expression on his face changed. He forbade the sadhus from writing a single word in response. As he teaches: "When you are praised, it feels great. You should feel the same when you are insulted. We are atma, not this body."

## Titiksha · Tolerance · તિતિક્ષા

### "Let the seasons do their duty"
Swamishri has braved freezing Sabarkantha winters and scorching Saurashtra summers to be with his devotees, telling them simply: "Let the seasons do their duty. We have to do ours." Until age 65, attendants had to force him to wear a warm cap; when devotees arranged air-conditioning in sweltering villages, he often refused it: "Why make it a habit? Better to tolerate the heat than depend on rare facilities."

### Walking to Dedvasan in the rain
In 1979, the dirt road to a remote tribal village became impassable — scrap metal, glass, jagged stones, and then a downpour. Before the sadhus could confer, Swamishri stepped out of the car and started walking. When one begged him to turn back for his health, he replied: "A little walking in the rain isn't going to hurt me. These devotees live here in these conditions, and you sadhus travel here all year tolerating heat, mosquitoes and hardships. Why shouldn't I?"

### Ten kilometres of mud at midnight
Driving to Sarangpur through torrential rain in 1967, the jeep slid into an irrigation channel at midnight — and the fuel tank, they discovered, was bone dry. Without a trace of irritation, Swamishri told the driver to wait with the vehicle, took his sandals in one hand and his cloth bag in the other, and walked ten kilometres through the mud, reaching Sarangpur at 3 a.m. — then sent fuel back for the jeep.

### The fever and the thal
Burning with what would be diagnosed as jaundice, Swamishri was carried barely conscious to a bed in Kapura — where he began mumbling. Leaning close, the sadhus made out his words: he was worried he had offended his host by ending the assembly early, and that a devotee who had come from another village hadn't received his visit. At 11:30 he forced himself upright with one question: "Have you prepared Thakorji's thal? Make sure you offer it on time." Not one word about his own suffering.

### The thorn
At a groundbreaking ceremony in 1984, a four-inch thorn drove through Swamishri's slipper and lodged deep in his foot. Before the sadhus could reach him, he had pulled it out himself, blood flowing. His first instruction: "Do not tell anyone." When sadhus later moved to scold the organizers for the uncleared ground, he stopped them: "There is no point in blaming anyone. You are bound to get hurt sometimes — you can't control such things."

### Laughing on the angiography table
On 7 July 1998, moments after doctors emerged grim-faced to announce he needed emergency bypass surgery, Swamishri lay still wired to the machines — watching his non-English-speaking attendant sadhu and an American doctor fumble hilariously to communicate. He burst out laughing. Told of the surgery, he said only, "Whatever God wishes," turned his mala, and fell soundly asleep for two hours while they prepared the operating theatre.

### John the physiotherapist
Recovering from bypass surgery, Swamishri finally admitted his shoulder had "been hurting for some time" — he had never mentioned it. The physiotherapist explained: "I'll press gradually harder; tell me to stop when you can't bear it." Swamishri nodded — and stayed silent even as the therapist's own thumb began to hurt from the force of pressing. He had never had a patient like this.

### "It's getting better"
Two months after the bypass, a fellow bypass patient — the entrepreneur Sam Pitroda — described the heaviness in the chest, "like a brick hanging from your throat. Do you feel it?" "Yes, I feel it. But it's getting better," Swamishri agreed easily. The attendant sadhus froze: he had never once mentioned it to any of them. He had simply carried it, in silence.

### "How much more grace do I need?"
Diagnosed with arthritis in his heel, Swamishri laughed and sang an old bhajan: "This body is old now, this body is old…" When a sadhu began to say he had endured enough, Swamishri cut in: "I haven't endured anything. Everyone must endure a little pain. I can sleep well, I can digest what I eat, I am not bedridden. What else can I ask for? How much more grace do I need from God?"

### The silence and the smile
Doctors examining a worsening heart condition urged him to stop his travels. Swamishri said nothing. "You are listening to me, right?" the cardiologist pressed. Swamishri simply flashed an innocent smile — and changed the subject. In Kolkata in 1993, ordered to rush to Mumbai over dizzy spells, he refused: "The devotees have worked hard to plan these events. Let us complete the schedule. Nothing will happen." It didn't.

### "Tolerate till the very end"
Sadhus once asked him: you always tell us to endure — but for how long? Isn't it sometimes right to respond? His answer distilled a lifetime: "Keep tolerating. There is no end to it. Respect and love even those who offend you. If you tolerate their insults, one day they will realize their mistakes — you can change them without saying a word. One who remains silent achieves all. Tolerate till the very end."

### "Every incident in my life is joyful"
A newspaper bureau chief asked whether he had any regrets in life. "Regret? For what?" Swamishri replied. "I act according to God's wish and realize he is responsible for everything — so I have no regrets." And what moment did he cherish most? "I enjoy everything. Every incident in my life is joyful. God is the source of all joy."

## Viraktihi · Detachment · વિરક્તિ:

### "Old is gold"
In New Jersey, two youths drove Swamishri in a top-of-the-line luxury car, hoping he would accept it for his village travels. "This car would be perfect for you, right?" Swamishri deflected: "I am fine with whatever we have in India. Old is gold!" They pressed — he builds grand new mandirs, why not a new car? "Mandirs are made for God and benefit thousands. This car would only give me comfort." They insisted it was their deep wish. "Never wish for such things."

### The helicopter suggestion
A youth suggested buying a helicopter to ease Swamishri's exhausting travel schedule. "One should desire liberation, not worldly comforts," Swamishri replied. When a well-built sadhu joked that he at least needed a bigger car — "I can barely fit in yours" — Swamishri laughed: "Rather than changing the car, you need to lose a little weight! Condition the mind and body to be comfortable anywhere — able to sleep equally on a dusty road or a king-size bed."

### "My hands are busy"
On the Eurostar from London to Paris, a devotee declared that the government should hand its power to Swamishri — he would do a great job. Swamishri, quietly turning his mala, raised his right hand slightly to show it and said: "My hands are busy turning the mala. I have no time for anything else." Offered a role fixing a corruption-plagued dam project, his answer was the same: "For a sadhu, devotion, mala and service are his duties."

### Refusing the Nobel Prize
When senior devotees began researching his nomination for the Nobel Peace Prize, Swamishri stopped them cold: "I have Bhagwan Swaminarayan and Gunatitanand Swami. That is the greatest Nobel Prize." A sadhu argued the award would spread his message to thousands. "If we offer devotion to God and sincerely serve society, people will benefit from our teachings anyway. Worldly awards do not guarantee true prestige. Stop thinking about the award — it is best to worship God."

### "This is our home, not a guest visit"
Arriving at the Vidyanagar student hostel to find the whole campus lavishly decorated with flowers for his visit, Swamishri turned to the sadhus: "There is no need for such extensive decorations when we come. It's not as if we are guests here — this is our home. Decorate like this only if there is a special occasion." Offered a decorated-elephant procession in Kutch, his answer was three words repeated: "No, no, no."

### The birthday he forgets
In 1968 Yogiji Maharaj instructed that Swamishri's birthday be celebrated grandly every year — and out of reverence for his guru's wish, Swamishri has attended ever since. But moments after thousands finish celebrating him, he walks off the stage and forgets it ever happened. The celebration is his guru's agna; the forgetting is entirely his own.

### The Prince's puzzle
Prince Philip, awestruck by Akshardham in Gandhinagar, assumed its creator must live there — and learned Swamishri only occasionally visits. Receiving Swamishri at Buckingham Palace the next year, the Prince said it aloud: "I am surprised you have made such a beautiful place, yet do not live there." Swamishri listened attentively — and just smiled. He builds for God and for generations; he keeps nothing, not even an address.

### "I don't have a hometown"
Youths once tried to coax Swamishri into nostalgia about Chansad, his native village. He didn't let them finish: "I don't have a hometown. I don't have a home or any relatives. I have God, and that is enough." In all his decades leading BAPS, he has never once used his influence or the organization's resources for his birth family or village.

### "God is calling!"
A devotee from London blurted out that Swamishri looked thin, wrinkled, unwell. Swamishri pulled him close and whispered with a grin: "That's what happens when you get old. God is calling!" Then, gently: "Don't worry about my body — I am bound to get sick. Talk about the atma. It is eternal; it never gets old. Focus on your atma and you will always be at peace."

### "I am not interested in anything either"
In Prague in 1994, as the group left to see a famous church, Swamishri offered his correspondence sadhu the chance to join them. "I'm not interested in sightseeing," the sadhu said, "I'll stay and catch up on letters." Swamishri answered instinctively, revealing his whole inner world in one line: "I am not interested in anything either — except God."

## Jnanam · Wisdom · જ્ઞાનમ્

### The dream in Sussex
Three English youths exploring Hinduism toured India without ever meeting Swamishri. Back home, one of them — David — saw Swamishri in a dream, where he was shown the experience of communion with God. The friends traced the face to the Neasden mandir, and days later stood before Swamishri himself. "Do you constantly experience that bliss in everything you do?" David asked. "Yes," Swamishri replied. "While I walk, talk, and in everything else I do, I have God with me."

### The brain scan that failed three times
During a 1993 brain scan, the technician grew frustrated: three attempts, and each time he begged Swamishri to "relax and calm his mind" for a clean reading. That night, when told the test must be repeated, Swamishri flatly refused — and finally explained why: "I was calm the whole time. I was imagining myself sitting in the Akshar Deri, meditating on Bhagwan Swaminarayan. Is that not serene enough?" The machine had simply never met a mind like his.

### "There is no birth or death"
Two visiting sannyasins asked Swamishri about his birthday celebrations: "Does that mean you were born?" His answer came with quiet confidence: "From a worldly standpoint, yes. Spiritually, there is no birth or death. I am Atma — eternal and indestructible." When an American professor asked him simply "Who are you?", the whole philosophy came back in one line: "I am Aksharbrahman." Replying to a letter, he once even wrote it in English, in three words: "I am Brahman."

### "As if you two are standing in front of me"
After Swamishri performed arti at the Akshar Deri, two children caught him despite his rushed schedule: "How do you experience God when you perform the arti?" Swamishri listened attentively and gave an answer no scholar could improve: "I experience God as if he is standing right in front of me — just like the two of you are standing in front of me." A teenager once asked how often he feels the bliss of Akshardham. One word: "Always."

### "Wherever I go is home"
A London youth urged him at dinner: "London is like your home — you should never leave." Swamishri smiled: "Friend, wherever I go is home. Whether I am in a palace or a villager's hut, I am always at home — we are from Akshardham." Phoned in Nairobi and asked when he was "coming back," he answered with a laugh: "I don't come or go. I am here in Nairobi and there in Ahmedabad as well. I am everywhere."

### Never lonely, never busy
Days after tens of thousands thronged a festival, a devotee found Swamishri sitting with just five people and asked if he felt lonely. "I have God with me, so I am never lonely. Without God, you would feel lonely even with all the people in the world." Another time a sadhu remarked how busy he always was. "I am never busy doing work," Swamishri corrected. "I am always worshipping Bhagwan Swaminarayan."

### The greatest technology
As Sri Sri Ravi Shankar was leaving Akshardham, Swamishri presented him with a framed photo taken just a short while earlier during the visit — edited, printed and framed before he departed. His guest marvelled at the speed. Then came the gentle upgrade: "This is the magic of technology — but the greatest technology is of atma and Paramatma. There is nothing greater than that."

### One niyam that contains all
A sadhu leaving for Chicago asked for a spiritual rule to follow until they met again. "The greatest niyam is believing yourself to be atma," Swamishri said. "All niyams are included in that — maintain it even when facing difficulty." Another sadhu, assigned to fundraising, asked for specific instructions. The entire briefing: "Believe yourself and others to be atma. That is all you have to do in life."

### "You know you are not a fool"
Told that a volunteer had to swallow insults while getting mandir work done, Swamishri gave the armor: "Someone might call you a fool. Stop and think — you know you are not a fool. One who understands the atma is not bothered by insults. Shastriji Maharaj faced terrible abuse; those people are gone, and his legacy lives on. Master this jnana and you will live peacefully."

### The writer on the edge
In Los Angeles, an acclaimed writer named Brian came to Swamishri carrying depression and several suicide attempts. "Who are we? What is the meaning of life?" he asked. Swamishri answered with tenderness: "We are not on this earth by accident. We are atma — the body will perish, but the atma lives on. Our goal is to liberate it by living peaceful, loving, moral lives that hurt no being." Brian left with something he hadn't arrived with: direction.

### Bhagvanjibhai's last letters
Diagnosed with blood cancer, Canadian community leader Bhagvanjibhai carried on so cheerfully that callers couldn't tell if he was phoning from his office or his deathbed. Days before passing at 43, he wrote to his brother: "Never fear death. Pramukh Swami Maharaj is a Gunatit Sadhu — don't ever think of leaving him." And to a spiritual leader: "I have a fatal illness, yet I am content, because I have had the chance to serve an ideal sadhu." He died with his face gleaming.

### "You took her to Akshardham"
A farmer named Chhaganbhai arranged his wife's funeral rites and then immediately left for his assigned mandir seva in Junagadh — convinced she had reached Akshardham, for she had said as much the day before. Swamishri phoned him at once and sent him home: "You must stay until all her rites are finished. You have to carry out your worldly duties as a husband. Both you and your wife have achieved a great understanding of satsang."

## Damaha · Self-Mastery · દમ:

### Nine months of a beginner's cooking
On the 1959 Africa tour, young Arunbhai cooked for the sadhus — bland food, beginner's skill. Most sadhus preferred it mild; Pramukh Swami famously enjoyed spice. For nine months he ate whatever was served without one comment or suggestion, even praising the food to encourage the novice cook. "That was the first time I realized he was no ordinary person," recalled the cook — who later became a senior sadhu himself.

### "Whatever is in his thal is prasad"
In over two decades of serving Swamishri's meals, his attendant cannot recall a single request for a particular dish. When children ask his favourite food, the answer never changes: "I like everything that has been offered to Harikrishna Maharaj." When cooking errors slip through, he stops the apology mid-sentence: "If Harikrishna Maharaj was offered the same food, why should I have a problem eating it?"

### The spoiled kadhi
In Cardiff in 1990, a rushed attendant accidentally made kadhi with sour milk instead of yogurt — it was foul enough that the sadhu who tasted it spat it out. Swamishri had already drunk his serving, and a second helping, without a flicker of displeasure. As the horrified attendant began apologizing, Swamishri smiled: "It really isn't a big deal. Harikrishna Maharaj was offered the same kadhi, and I have no problem eating his prasad."

### Rotlis with stones in them
An overjoyed devotee brought flour from his brand-new hand-mill — forgetting that a new mill's first grindings carry grit. The rotlis went to Swamishri first. Only when the sadhus later bit into their own did they discover the tiny stones throughout. Swamishri had eaten his entire meal without the slightest change of expression — protecting a devotee's joy with his silence.

### The sugarcane juice
A sadhu arrived with fresh sugarcane juice, and Swamishri stopped him at the door: "I told you not to bring any yesterday, and you have brought it again. Take it back." The sadhus coaxed — just a little, he won't bring more tomorrow. Swamishri smiled but held firm: "No. If I have some today, he is sure to bring some tomorrow." His lifelong rule: "Don't accept everything you are given. Learn to accept only what you need."

### The six-year-old who wouldn't eat
At a children's feast in Bhadran, the host noticed young Shantilal slip away from the dining area. "Today is Ekadashi and I am fasting," the six-year-old explained. Fifty children and an insistent host tried to persuade him; he began to cry rather than break the fast. The host had to bow before the conviction of a child — a conviction that never once bent in the following eighty years.

### 122 homes with a 102-degree fever
Until 57, Swamishri observed dharna-parna — eating only every other day — through the four months of chaturmas, and waterless fasts on every Ekadashi, never once easing his schedule for it. One day he visited 122 homes while fasting with a 102-degree fever. After his heart attack at 63, attendants begged him to take food with his medicine on fast days. The stern reply: "I am not going to die if I don't eat for one day!"

### The eclipse dinner
Scripture exempts those over 80 from eclipse fasting rules, but in New Delhi in 2006, 85-year-old Swamishri refused his evening meal anyway. His attendant of twenty years tried every angle — it's offered to Harikrishna Maharaj, it's prasad, breakfast will be so late. Each attempt met a smiling wall: "If Harikrishna Maharaj has eaten, the benefit has been attained. I am not going to starve if I skip one meal." Since 2007 he has quietly given up lunch altogether.

### "The greatest celibate of our times"
At an assembly in Dubai, Acharya Pushparaj — from a different tradition entirely — rose to say what he had observed: "Even the devas bow to a man who follows eight-fold brahmacharya for a single day. Pramukh Swami Maharaj has observed it his entire life. I am not exaggerating when I say he is the greatest celibate of our times." Sixty-nine years as a sadhu; a character without one blemish.

## Aishvaryam · Divine Power · ઐશ્વર્યમ્

### "Nothing to say. Nothing to say."
R.K. Laxman — India's most celebrated political cartoonist, the nation's boldest critic — visited Akshardham for two days at the President's urging and then met its inspirer. Mid-sentence, the famously sharp voice simply stopped. His lips moved; no words came. Tears ran from the corners of his eyes as he gazed at Swamishri, until he finally managed to repeat three words: "Nothing to say. Nothing to say." His wife said she had never seen her husband so moved in his life.

### "Surrounded by angels"
Dr. Faisal Zeera, a devout Muslim and president of the International Hospital of Bahrain, calls Swamishri his guru: "The first time I met him, I felt as if he was surrounded by angels and liberated souls. I felt I was in the presence of the ultimate divinity — an experience of the soul, not of the mind. And it was not a one-time experience. I have felt that divinity every single time."

### The surgeon and the heart
Asked on television whether 20,000 heart operations had ever produced a special experience, Dr. V. Subramanium paused: "There was one. When I took Pramukh Swami Maharaj's heart in my hand, I felt a sense of divinity all around — as if some divine being had entered my operation theatre. I have never experienced that in any other operation." And when Swamishri had left Lenox Hill after his week of recovery in 1998, the entire cardiothoracic staff had lined his exit to bid their patient farewell.

### Jashbhai's one condition
Jashbhai, headman of Sokhda-Matar, despised sadhus and lent his house for their stay on one condition: "Don't ask me to come for darshan." Swamishri, staying there, insisted on thanking the unseen host. Dragged reluctantly to the meeting, Jashbhai took one look at Swamishri — and fell at his feet. "Will you stay a few more days? I want a chance to serve you." He remained a devoted servant for the rest of his life, never quite able to explain what changed in that first glance.

### The wrong exit
A top New York surgeon, driving Swamishri behind a police escort, was startled when his passenger interrupted their conversation: "This road doesn't take us where we want to go. We should have taken the last exit." Dr. Mehta had driven those roads for twenty years — and Swamishri was right. The next day he told visiting sadhus: "I have met Pope John Paul II and many religious leaders, but Swamishri is different. The only word I can think of is divine."

### The shark off Karachi
Retrieving a child's drifting tube 300 feet from shore, young Dutch traveller Han Kop turned to find a shark closing in. Splashing only drew it nearer; his life began flashing before his eyes. Then, he says, Pramukh Swami Maharaj — whom he had met just days earlier in Gondal — appeared before him. He let go of the tube and swam; for five minutes he saw neither water nor waves, only Swamishri's form. "There is no way I could have survived," he says, "if it wasn't for Swamishri."

### The letter that arrived late
Canada's former Solicitor General, Bob Kaplan, once requested Swamishri's blessings for Pierre Trudeau's election campaign. The blessing letter — predicting Trudeau would again become Prime Minister — arrived only after the election was lost. Trudeau read it with a wry smile and set it aside. Months later, a political crisis toppled the government, and Trudeau became Prime Minister after all. Kaplan tells the story publicly to this day.

### Valio of the hills
Valio was a drunken thug whose gang terrorized tribal Sabarkantha — police once found 50 kilos of raw meat at the scene of a livestock heist. The world had written him off. Then he met Swamishri. Today he is Valabhai, tilak-chandlo on his forehead, and all sixty of his men and their families live devout, honest lives. "We promised Pramukh Swami Maharaj we would never steal again," he says — and on the hills of his village, a small tape deck plays the Swaminarayan dhun.

### The rickshaw driver's honesty
Barkubhai once robbed villages in broad daylight with a machete. After meeting Swamishri, he drives a rickshaw. When a father shopping for his daughter's wedding left a bag full of cash behind, Barkubhai spent half the day finding him to return it — then refused a reward, and refused the offered night of drinks: "I quit long ago. I have met a pure guru like Pramukh Swami Maharaj. My only goal is to become brahmarup and go to Akshardham."

### "You came with your father in 1959"
Subhashbhai of Dar-es-Salaam was so violent even the police feared him. Dragged to Swamishri's darshan in 1995, he heard the sadhu say: "I know you. You came for Yogiji Maharaj's darshan with your father in 1959." Thirty-six years, one meeting, remembered perfectly. Something in that recognition undid him. Today he performs puja and arti daily — a transformation that startled even his old classmate, the President of Tanzania.

### Ramsang Bapu's departure
Ramsang Bapu was such a feared fighter that a national gangster offered him half his profits as a partner. Swamishri visited his home in Odarka, spoke to him about the harvest of violence — and the terror of the region became a devotee for the rest of his days. On 13 October 1997, Swamishri promised him that Bhagwan Swaminarayan would take him to Akshardham soon. Thirteen days later he passed away telling his son: "Bhagwan Swaminarayan and Pramukh Swami Maharaj have come to take me. I am going…"

### "Miracles do not lead to moksha"
Countless devotees credit Swamishri's blessings with the impossible. His own response never varies: "All I do is pray. Bhagwan Swaminarayan listens and helps the devotees. But miracles do not lead to moksha — they are a hindrance, raising body-consciousness. Faithfully worship God instead." And when a doctor concluded "I believe you to be God," Swamishri shook his head: "No, no. I am God's humble servant. God works through a sadhu."

## Shauryam · Courage · શૌર્યમ્

### "Please forgive me"
In 1981, during Bhagwan Swaminarayan's bicentenary celebrations in Ahmedabad, the Bharat Sadhu Samaj planned its Silver Jubilee gathering in Swamishri's presence, and its secretary suggested honouring a female national leader on stage. Swamishri answered gently, "You can do that, but according to the niyams of celibacy prescribed by Bhagwan Swaminarayan, I will not be able to attend the event. Please forgive me." He held the same line while meeting President Clinton, President Nyerere of Tanzania and Kofi Annan, while addressing the United Nations — even during his own emergency bypass surgery. His courage never insulted anyone; it simply never bent.

### "We will sit in a corner and worship God"
The social reformer Ishwarbhai Petlikar told Swamishri in 1981 that if he wanted his organization to grow, he should relax the celibacy niyams for his sadhus. Swamishri was direct: "I do not believe in growing by transgressing the niyams laid down by Bhagwan Swaminarayan. There is no need for that type of growth." Many leaders had warned that the organization would not last long because of its stringent niyams. "So be it," Swamishri said. "We will sit in a corner and worship God, but we will not sacrifice our niyams." The Sanstha grew across the world — without sacrificing a single one.

### "Whether people like it or not"
Former Finance Minister of India H.M. Patel once suggested that Swamishri soften the sadhus' niyams, and the journalist Kanti Bhatt asked him plainly whether he had ever thought of changing the celibacy niyams "to cater for the modern way of thinking." Swamishri replied, "Things are fine the way they are. There is nothing to change. People might call us orthodox, but it is our duty to follow the niyams given to us by Bhagwan Swaminarayan. It's not like we are insulting or hurting anyone by following them. So whether people like it or not, we have to follow Bhagwan Swaminarayan's agna."

### Strict with himself, generous with the world
Though Swamishri never once relaxed his own niyams, he worked tirelessly for women's education, health and spiritual growth. Dr Hanna Kim, a Ph.D. from New York University who has studied the BAPS Women's Wing for fifteen years, marvelled at the devotion, creativity and leadership the organization nurtured in women — "all possible because of Pramukh Swami Maharaj's blessings." In tribal villages Swamishri pressed for girls' schooling: "Educating a woman helps to educate the whole family. Education gives independence and vision." He proved that keeping one's own discipline and empowering others are not opposites — they are the same courage.

### Standing up for Rama
A popular singer once performed in a satsang assembly in Ahmedabad and, hoping to amuse the audience, made demeaning jokes about Bhagwan Rama and Sitaji while praising the demon king Ravan. When Swamishri rose to bless the assembly, he set the record straight before everyone: "Bhagwan Rama was God and Sitaji was his ideal devotee. Their lives were perfect. Sitaji stayed loyal to Bhagwan Rama despite numerous temptations. Ravan was evil and immoral — he is not an appropriate role model for anyone!" He never shamed the singer personally, but he would not let God be belittled for a laugh.

### "Has the Bible sanctioned gambling?"
Catholic Bishop John Markovsky told Swamishri in Houston that bingo sessions helped fund the schools and hospitals his diocese ran. Swamishri asked quietly, "Has the Bible sanctioned gambling?" The Bishop admitted it had not. "Then how can you promote gambling in the name of God?" Swamishri continued. "If religious institutions sanction gambling, children and youths will think it is okay to gamble. Religion is supposed to prevent people from such activities." The Bishop conceded, "You have set the bar very high, but we will try to follow your ideals."

### "Make them true Christians"
In a 1980 dialogue, Swamishri asked the Archbishop of Canterbury how many Christians in the world flawlessly follow the Ten Commandments. "Very few, Swamiji," the Archbishop admitted. "Then wouldn't it make more sense to focus on making those Christians true or perfect Christians?" Swamishri reasoned. "What's the point of focusing on conversions? If every person follows his or her religion properly, world harmony would become a reality." He himself built hundreds of mandirs to preserve Hindu culture — yet without a hint of force or insult toward any other faith.

### "The bravest soul on earth"
At 85, Swamishri inaugurated Swaminarayan Akshardham in New Delhi; at 87 he was still consecrating traditional mandirs outside India. D.R. Karthikeyan, former Director of India's CBI and former Director General of the National Human Rights Commission, was awestruck: "I don't think any king, government or international organization can build Akshardham. God is working through him. This sadhu has given up everything so that he can give back to humanity. You need courage to build something so majestic — I think he is the most courageous man I have ever met."

## Smrutihi · Loving Remembrance · સ્મૃતિઃ

### "I know his entire lineage"
In 2006 in Vidyanagar, a hostel student named Mitesh was being introduced to Swamishri, and the sadhus asked the boy to name his father and grandfather. Swamishri stopped them mid-sentence: "I know his entire lineage. Your name is Mitesh. Your father is Ramesh, Ramesh's father was Narsinh, and Narsinh's father was Narayanbhai — right?" He then recalled the family's seva in Shastriji Maharaj's time and even asked after their neighbour Gunjan by name. Oddly, just the day before, Swamishri had confessed to a sadhu, "I also have trouble remembering things." Not, it seems, when it came to his devotees.

### Forty-five years later
In Sarangpur in 1998, a devotee named Becharbhai was introduced as being from Rohishala. Swamishri kept asking, "Where did you live before you moved to Rohishala?" — even calling him back after he had walked past. "I used to live in Chakampar," Becharbhai finally said. Swamishri's eyes sparkled as he grabbed his hand: "Now I remember! We came to your house twice in Chakampar for padhramani." A sadhu asked Becharbhai how long ago those visits had been. "About 45 years!" he said — and had no answer for how Swamishri remembered.

### The boy who knew less than his guru
In London in 2004, a youth introduced himself: "My name is Haresh and I am from Shrijipura." Swamishri asked his father's name, then began telling the sadhus the whole story — how the family originally came from Ugamedi, how Shastriji Maharaj had brought them to Shrijipura to care for the Sanstha's property, the names of his father, uncle and great-uncles, the brother who was a sadhu in Sarangpur, even their relatives in Surat. Haresh stood frozen with the microphone in his hand. Swamishri knew more about his family than he did.

### A wedding blessed in 1959
In 2004, Bhanubhai Patel of London brought an elderly relative for Swamishri's darshan. Swamishri turned to a sadhu and explained, "This is Bhanubhai's father-in-law. His name is Somabhai. We had gone to Kampala in 1959 to bless Bhanubhai on the day of his wedding." The 87-year-old Somabhai was nearly in tears — Swamishri had remembered him across forty-five years and two continents.

### Mohan, the stonecutter
In 1995 in Sarangpur, Swamishri spotted an elderly farmer among the devotees: "Mohan! How are you?" He took Mohan's hand, led him into his own room and seated him like a VIP guest, introducing him to all the sadhus: "Mohan is an old devotee. Shastriji Maharaj kept him with us while building the mandir in Atladra. He was an expert in stone cutting." Mohan's failing memory and toothless mouth made his words hard to follow, but Swamishri listened attentively to every one, then retold Mohan's stories to the others himself. Watching from the side, Mohan's son could not hold back his tears.

### "Call out the jay nad"
In Chicago in 2007, Swamishri met a devotee from Detroit and asked about his native village. "Paliyad," the man said. "You are from Paliyad? One of our devotees lives there — Govindbhai Shivabhai," said Swamishri. "That's my father!" Swamishri smiled: "Govindbhai has a lot of energy. He can rattle windows with his jay nad. Why don't you do the same? Call out the jay nad!" The devotee hailed the jay nad with all his strength, and Swamishri laughed and blessed him with a pat on the back.

### The car that turned toward Narsanda
A sadhu once told Swamishri not to bother visiting Gordhanbhai of Narsanda — "It's not worth it" — because he had once turned two promised padhramanis into fifty while Swamishri had a fever. Swamishri was quiet a moment, then firm: "Do you know how much he has done for Shastriji Maharaj? No one would let us into Narsanda, and he bravely opposed everyone so that Shastriji Maharaj could stay in the village mandir. Never say it's not worth it." A few days later, driving back from Vartal, Swamishri turned the car toward Narsanda unannounced. Gordhanbhai, sitting on the swing in his yard, wept with joy as Swamishri walked in and said simply, "You have done so much for Shastriji Maharaj."

### A rose behind the pillar
In Gondal in 1994, Swamishri was doing darshan at the Akshar Deri while Govindsinh Chudasama sat behind one of the pillars, virtually out of sight. While circling the shrine, Swamishri glanced at him from the corner of his eye. Finishing darshan, he picked up a rose from the Akshar Deri, walked to that corner, bent down, and placed it in Govindsinh's lap. Govindsinh was left trying to figure out when Swamishri had even seen him.

### Two sadhus walked instead
In Dharmaj, C.M. Patel of London took the last seat in a car heading back to the mandir, and a devotee was about to ask him to walk the short distance instead. Swamishri immediately disagreed — he told two sadhus to get out and walk so that C.M. Patel would not have to. On the way, Swamishri quietly explained why: C.M. Patel had arthritis and could not have managed even that short walk. He carried the needs of thousands in his heart — whose room must be on the ground floor, who must not be served sweets, who should never be made to sit on the floor.

### "No one lets me in"
In Sarangpur in 2006, Swamishri recognized a face among 150 devotees: "That's Bhagvat Joshi from Anand" — a man who had travelled with Yogiji Maharaj in the 1950s. "Where have you been? Why didn't you come to meet me in Bochasan?" Swamishri asked, again and again. Bhagvat finally admitted, "It's so difficult to meet you nowadays. No one lets me in." A sadhu offered to escort him in next time, but Swamishri brushed that aside: "Why do you need to bring him? He can come by himself." The old devotee was left in tears at the belonging in Swamishri's voice.

### The evening he praised every sadhu
On 30 August 1998, recovering from bypass surgery, Swamishri finished his evening walk in a New York park and began praising the sadhu who had written Yogiji Maharaj's biography. Then he kept going — sadhu after sadhu, senior and junior, pujaris and cooks, musicians and housekeepers, naming the special quality of each one. Dusk fell, the park emptied, and Swamishri kept on — through the walk to the car, the whole drive home, and fifteen more minutes inside the house; even after lying down to rest he called a sadhu over to recount the virtues of seven more. He praised over sixty sadhus for more than ninety minutes — men scattered across Gujarat who had no idea their guru was singing their glory. When the sadhus protested that all of it was really his own doing, he refused the credit: "This is Yogiji Maharaj's grace." He paused only once more, to say, "We should always see the good in others. That is the only way to inner peace."

## Kaushalam · Excellence · કૌશલમ્

### The cricket captain who walked away
As a boy in Chansad, Shantilal was one of the best swimmers in the village — he hardly ever lost a race at the village lake — ranked first in his class, and captained the village cricket team. The team had collected about five hundred rupees — a large sum in that era — and trusted the teenager to buy new cricket equipment. On the very day he was to make the purchase, a letter arrived from his guru Shastriji Maharaj, calling him to become a sadhu. That was all it took: he handed everything over and left. There would be no more races to win or maths tests to ace — his excellence would now lead thousands toward God.

### The president who didn't want the post
Shastriji Maharaj saw young Narayanswarupdasji's quiet proficiency and placed him on the Sanstha's board at 23, made him Kothari of Sarangpur at 24, and in 1950 appointed the 28-year-old as Pramukh — president — of the entire organization. Swamishri at first declined the post; he disliked authority and designations and wanted only to serve as a sevak. He accepted only at his guru's repeated insistence, and even then never demanded respect or wielded his title, working silently under Yogiji Maharaj's guidance. Under that reluctant president, the Sanstha soared to heights no one had imagined.

### The sculptor's eye
When new murtis are being carved, Swamishri reviews them personally, and even master sculptors are stunned by what he catches at a glance: "The ear on the left side of this murti is a bit too high… the left shoulder on this one is too thin… the cheeks on this murti aren't full…" He is involved in a mandir from the first day — choosing the land, the stone, the design of the inner shrine — and his exacting eye has revived the ancient art of building mandirs according to the Shilpashastras. Along the way, he has trained sadhus into some of the finest murti sculptors and mandir builders of the age.

### "The credit goes to you"
Robert Blake Jr., once named the US government's best Deputy Chief of Mission worldwide, saw Swaminarayan Akshardham in New Delhi and couldn't wait to meet its creator. "You are not just an outstanding spiritual leader — you are a great leader," he told Swamishri. "You motivated thousands of people to work together and finish this masterpiece in just five years. The credit goes to you." Swamishri's reply was immediate: "All the credit belongs to Bhagwan Swaminarayan and my guru Yogiji Maharaj." He built the world's largest Hindu mandir and kept none of the glory.

### "I can barely manage five employees"
In Junagadh in 2006, an orthopaedic surgeon, Dr Prashant Chhaya, confessed to Swamishri, "I can barely manage the five employees in my clinic. How do you manage this gigantic Sanstha?" "God manages everything," Swamishri replied in one sentence. The doctor pressed — surely God works through him? Swamishri explained, "God is the all-doer. If you believe in that divine concept, you can inspire everyone. If we understand everyone's greatness, cooperate with them and work to please God, everything falls into place."

### "Some good will always come of it"
Dr Raymond Williams, a professor of religion, once tested Swamishri: "You are a sadhu with no experience of social life. How can you guide devotees in worldly matters?" "God gives me the answers," Swamishri said. "God has experienced everything." The professor pushed further — what if a devotee bought a business on his advice and lost everything? Wouldn't he regret the answer? "No, of course not. God knows your past, present and future. He does everything for a reason. Some good will always come of it." God sits at the core of his every decision — which is why he is always relaxed, at ease, and unshaken.

## Dhairyam · Patience · ધૈર્યમ્

### The seat on the bathroom stairs
In Dhari, Swamishri had stepped out of an assembly and had just come out of the shower when a devotee approached him, desperate to talk at once. Swamishri was due back on stage, but he heard the pain in the man's voice — so he sat down right there, on the stairs outside the bathroom. There was barely any room, fresh air or even light, but that didn't bother him. He listened for fifteen minutes as the devotee emptied his heart out, and did not get up until he had given him a satisfying solution.

### Blessings from the recovery room
Hours after Swamishri's bypass surgery in New York, a hospital attendant named Robert lingered by his bed. "I want to explain my situation to him," Robert told the sadhus. "I am sure my marriage plans will work out if he gives me his blessings." Swamishri, barely awake from the anaesthetic, blessed him. Seven days a week, 365 days a year, for decades — the meeting of devotees has never stopped, not even in a surgical ward.

### "Helping devotees is bhakti"
In 2006, a sadhu phoned Swamishri from London and asked what he was up to. "I was reading and writing a few letters," came the answer — as it had for forty years, across more than 500,000 letters. "You've spent your whole life writing letters," the sadhu said. "I would be bored of reading the same problems over and over. I would go crazy." Swamishri replied, "There is nothing to be bored of. Helping devotees is bhakti — it is part of my devotion to Bhagwan Swaminarayan. Don't go crazy! Happiness or sorrow, you must patiently get through any situation."

### Reading letters with one eye
In March 2006, an eye infection left Swamishri's eyes swollen and throbbing; the doctors padded the infected eye to protect the other. Still he met devotees all day and read letters in the gaps between them — one letter alone took him half an hour to finish. When a sadhu protested that one long, barely legible letter wasn't worth the strain, Swamishri disagreed: "That devotee must be suffering so much. It must have taken him a long time to write it. The least we should do is read it." The sadhu groaned, "He is in pain? We are all in pain just watching you read that letter!" Swamishri laughed: "You try to help, and it still seems to trouble others!"

### "I want to file a complaint!"
In Surat in 1995, a 78-year-old devotee pushed forward and announced, "I want to file a complaint! Why did God take my wife before me?" His voice climbed as he blamed Swamishri for not letting her live until he could reach her hospital bed — "They even delay an execution for a final death wish!" The crowd grew agitated, but Swamishri stayed calm and consoled him gently: "She was a true devotee — she is in Akshardham, and she never had to live a single day without you. It would have been far harder for her the other way around." The old man kept accusing; Swamishri kept blessing him with the same love.

### The elephant and the barking dog
In New York in 1998, a devotee burst out that he couldn't bear the insults hurled at Swamishri by jealous critics: "You are so pure and innocent. I want to set them straight!" Swamishri stopped him with an image: "Imagine an elephant walking down a path, and a dog starts barking. The elephant doesn't stop — he keeps walking as if he hears nothing, confident and unperturbed. That is exactly how a true sadhu works." When another sadhu once asked whether he ever lost his temper at such insults, Swamishri answered simply, "Never. It has never bothered me."

### Teaching Bharat
Bharat, a mentally challenged youth with a stutter and a lisp, serves at the mandir in Bhadra, and his mixed-up answers to Swamishri's questions often made onlookers laugh out loud. Where others saw a joke, Swamishri saw a student. When Bharat answered that "Bhadra" was born in Bhadra, Swamishri corrected him with a smile — "Gunatitanand Swami was born here. Bhadra wasn't born in Bhadra!" — and had him repeat it five times, just as a caring mother teaches her child. Then, resting a hand lovingly on his head, he taught him the murtis one by one: "The first is Gunatitanand Swami, the second is Bhagwan Swaminarayan, the third is Gopalanand Swami — now repeat." Then he instructed the local sadhus to keep teaching Bharat the names of all the murtis. "At least he is willing to learn everything we teach him," Swamishri said. Nobody else believed Bharat could learn; Swamishri did.

### "I apologize. It was my fault."
In 1966, troublemakers stoned devotees' cars after a murti-pratishtha in Sardargadh that senior devotees had wanted postponed — a ceremony Yogiji Maharaj himself had insisted on holding. When the seniors angrily blamed Swamishri for going ahead, he folded his hands and said only, "I apologize. It was my fault." He never mentioned that it had been his guru's decision. The truth surfaced 23 years later, when a devotee who had witnessed it published the story. Reminded of it, Swamishri said, "My only goal was to please Yogiji Maharaj. As long as he was pleased, I didn't mind tolerating anyone's criticism."

### The Akshardham response
On 24 September 2002, terrorists stormed Swaminarayan Akshardham in Gandhinagar, gunning down innocent pilgrims — and one of Swamishri's own sadhus. When the phone call reached the 82-year-old Swamishri in Sarangpur, he was the calmest person in the room: "Let us pray to Bhagwan Swaminarayan that there is no more loss of life and that the terrorists are caught." Knowing Gujarat could erupt into communal riots, he made an immediate, soothing appeal for peace over television, radio and the papers — and the state stayed still. Strategists at an international anti-terrorism conference later named it "The Akshardham Response," calling it the most effective answer to a terror attack they had studied. Swamishri explained simply: "No amount of violence would have brought those departed souls back to life. Why lose more innocent lives? We are not supposed to retaliate — that is in God's hands."

## Kantihi · Divine Radiance · કાન્તિઃ

### Six hundred photographs, none rejected
Professor Jaroslav Fric of the Czech Republic — famed across Europe as the "Multimedia Man" — had met kings, queens, prime ministers and presidents, and of his photographs of kings and great men he would typically reject seventy percent because tension and worry crept into their faces. Then he photographed Pramukh Swami Maharaj while creating the multimedia show for Akshardham. "I took 600 photographs of Pramukh Swami and I could not reject any of them. Not one!" he marvelled, his eyes welling with tears. "In all his photographs I find nothing but absolute purity, divine peace and love. This is my life's greatest experience."

### The customs officer's double take
On 27 July 2007, a US customs officer named Carl boarded Swamishri's plane at Morris County Airport, New Jersey, to complete the entry formalities. The sadhus had told him to expect an 86-year-old sadhu, and he had braced himself for a weary old man. Instead his eyes fell on a face with a sparkle he couldn't comprehend and a glow that radiated peace and tranquility. Carl was stunned: "He looks at ease. There is something extraordinarily different about his face. He doesn't look 86."

### "Another hundred years"
Dr Raghunath Mashelkar, one of India's most celebrated scientists and a contributor to its supercomputer, came for his first darshan of Swamishri in New Delhi. The man of science left searching for words: "I touched Swamishri's feet and felt great peace. There is a tremendous divine glow that radiates from his face. I was so rejuvenated by his darshan, I felt as if someone had just added another hundred years to my lifespan."

### "Everything you see is because of God"
In Jamnagar in 2005, an orthopaedic surgeon, Dr V.M. Shah, told Swamishri, "Your face radiates with energy and splendour even at this age. And your eyes are so powerful that I feel attracted to them." "It is all God's grace," Swamishri replied. "Everything you see is because of God." The doctor insisted it was Swamishri's own effort that made everything possible. "That effort is Bhagwan Swaminarayan's blessings and my guru's grace," came the answer. His humility is his truest radiance.

## Mardavam · Tender Love · માર્દવમ્

### "Please forgive me for being direct"
At a spiritual camp in Karjat in 1994, Swamishri spoke to the participants about discipline with unusual firmness, saying everything that needed to be said. The sadhus noticed he was uneasy afterwards — sternness is simply not his nature. On the last day of the camp he addressed everyone: "Please forgive me for being very direct the other day. I apologize if I have said anything to offend or hurt you…" He couldn't finish the sentence; he was in tears. His first thought after any firm word has always been, "I hope I didn't hurt his feelings."

### A birthday spent apologizing
At his 75th birthday celebration in Mumbai, sadhus and devotees spent nearly three hours narrating incidents in praise of Swamishri — but he was thinking only of the thousands seated before him. "You have come from different corners of the world and must have been inconvenienced. I regret that I haven't been able to meet all of you personally… I am sorry," he said, fighting back tears and losing. "Though you are far from me physically, you are in my heart. I ask all of you to bless me so that I am able to please all of the sadhus and devotees." On his own birthday, he asked his devotees for their blessings.

### "It's me. Pramukh Swami."
As Swamishri was leaving Bochasan, every devotee crowded around for a final darshan — except one. Manibhai of Thasra sat alone in the corner, unmoving, for Manibhai was blind. Swamishri walked over to him and placed a hand on his head. "Who is it?" Manibhai asked — and heard the familiar, soothing voice: "It's me. Pramukh Swami." The old man cried his heart out as Swamishri lovingly caressed his head.

### The hut with cow-dung walls
Neemabhai, a tribal devotee of Chuli village on the banks of the Narmada, had waited years for Swamishri to grace his hut. In 1998, Swamishri ducked through the low doorway of the straw-roofed home with its cow-dung walls and floors, calling out, "Neemabhai! I am here. Come, let me bless your hut." Neemabhai offered the adadna vada he had prepared; Swamishri offered them to Harikrishna Maharaj and ate a small piece. Overjoyed and in tears, Neemabhai said, "How will I ever be able to repay your kindness? Your love is beyond all social barriers."

### The president who wouldn't leave
When former US President Bill Clinton visited Swaminarayan Akshardham in Gandhinagar in April 2001, he sat down on a plain sofa in the scorching afternoon sun, patted the seat beside him, and asked Swamishri to join him. "There are too many leaders who believe that by pulling others down you can bring yourself up," he told the sadhus, pointing at Swamishri. "But he is different. He connects. I saw in his eyes that he is a man who has not come ahead by eclipsing others — he has come forward by always placing others before him." His Secret Service detail kept hinting at the schedule, but Clinton held Swamishri's hands and would not let go. Finally rising, he sighed, "Just when you are starting to have the time of your life, you have to leave."

### "You are my father and you are my mother"
Ron Patel, the Sunday editor of the Philadelphia Inquirer, arrived skeptical and cynical when he first met Swamishri in 1991 — an atheist who had never believed in God. Swamishri invited him to visit the land of his father, organized his entire 1992 trip around India, and looked after him like a parent as he reconnected with his ancestral roots. After Swamishri's bypass surgery in 1998, Ron sent a get-well card: "You have given me the love of a father and a mother. You are my father and you are my mother. You are my family. May you live forever."

### The language of love
Chatur serves in the printing department at Swaminarayan Aksharpith in Ahmedabad. He was born deaf and mute — yet he understands everything Swamishri says, and Swamishri understands every one of Chatur's grunts and sighs. Sadhus and devotees gather just to watch the two of them converse, an exchange that has brought tears to many eyes. They speak the language of love, and in that language nothing is ever lost.

### Two days for a six-year-old
Six-year-old Abhay Jaiswal chatted freely with Swamishri during darshan and blurted out an invitation: "Why don't you come to Karvan? We will have so much fun together." In March 1997, Swamishri was in Por and mysteriously refused to tell anyone — not even his attendant sadhus — where the next two days would be spent. On the morning of departure he announced the destination: Karvan. He went straight to Abhay's house and dedicated those two days to the little boy. No one else had remembered the invitation — except, of course, Swamishri.

### A wedding gifted by a guru
Bhaskar Mehta lost his father as an infant, and Swamishri walked him through life in his father's place. When Bhaskar resolved to become a sadhu, Swamishri said no — "Who would take care of his mother?" — and instead found him a match, speaking with the bride's family and arranging the dates as any father would. When it emerged that Bhaskar couldn't afford a wedding, Swamishri called him: "Don't worry. I am coming to Bharuch soon — I will arrange your wedding while I am there, and we will feed all the guests along with the devotees." When the bride's brother hesitated to accept the Sanstha's help, Swamishri settled it gently: "Your father and uncle have done so much for this Sanstha, it is my duty to look after your family. Think of it as a gift from God."

### The night detour to Limbasi
Driving back to Bochasan from a cattle camp one night, Swamishri told his long-time driver to turn at an unfamiliar crossing. The driver knew this wasn't the road home, but complied — and the car wound through the narrow streets of Limbasi until Swamishri found the house of Dahyabhai, a devotee suffering from terminal cancer. At 9:00 p.m., Swamishri knocked on his door. "What are you doing in Limbasi this late at night?" the startled Dahyabhai asked. Swamishri simply smiled, walked in, placed his hand on Dahyabhai's head and blessed him — and Dahyabhai stood on his porch in tears, watching the car drive away.

### Forty-five minutes among the tombstones
In 1990, leaving Colchester, Swamishri made puzzling arrangements: two separate cars, with Harikrishna Maharaj and the sadhus sent back to the London mandir in the other one. He then directed his driver to south London — to an empty funeral home in the middle of a cemetery, where he sat on a bench for 45 minutes, reading the names on tombstones, waiting. Soon a small family arrived for the final rites of Jaykrishnabhai, a devotee whose father had served Swamishri during his student days in Bhadran. Days earlier, Jaykrishnabhai had phoned Swamishri with a dying wish: "Please take me to Akshardham. Come bless me at my cremation rites." Swamishri had given him his word — and so, unannounced, he performed the final rites, helped push the coffin into the crematorium, and kept his promise to the very end.

## Astikyam · Faith in God · આસ્તિક્યમ્

### "This is not a symbol"
In Zimbabwe in 1988, a TV reporter pointed at the small murti of Harikrishna Maharaj beside Swamishri and asked, "What is that a symbol of?" Swamishri corrected him gently: "This is not a symbol. This is Bhagwan Swaminarayan. He inspires us and guides us in everything that we do. God is omnipresent, but he is always present in this murti. We offer everything to him before we use it." The murti — worshipped by Gunatitanand Swami and later Yogiji Maharaj before him — travels wherever Swamishri goes, and his whole existence is centred around it.

### The guest of honour
When the Canadian Parliament honoured Swamishri in 1988, the Speaker announced his presence and requested him to stand. To everyone's surprise, Swamishri did not — he first had a sadhu stand up holding Thakorji, the murti of Bhagwan Swaminarayan, and only then rose briefly, folded his hands, and sat back down. In his mind, it wasn't he who was being honoured. Likewise, when the Mayor of Nairobi came to present the Key to the City, Swamishri led him by the hand to Thakorji and had him offer it there first — and only then accepted it.

### Thakorji boards first
In the early hours of 5 June 1977, hundreds of devotees gathered at Mumbai airport to see Swamishri off to London. The Airport Manager and the Captain escorted him to the aircraft and asked him to board first. Swamishri hesitated and looked around; the Captain requested again; Swamishri kept waiting — until Thakorji arrived, and the sadhu carrying the murti boarded first. Only then did Swamishri smile at the officers and step aboard. They were treating him like a celebrity, but in his eyes, Thakorji was the only celebrity on that flight.

### Soil in a cloth
In Atlanta in 1988, as Swamishri was leaving for an assembly, a devotee approached carrying soil from his property tied in a cloth — he was building a motel and wanted Swamishri to step on the soil and consecrate it. Instead, Swamishri called for Thakorji, sat down on the floor, took the murti into his hands and placed him upon the soil. He chanted the dhun and sprinkled flower petals, then told the devotee the motel would turn out perfectly — because Thakorji had consecrated it. He would not take for himself an honour that belonged to God.

### The only chair in the house
During padhramanis in Vadadala, Swamishri arrived at a devotee's home that had just one seat. He immediately had the sadhus place Thakorji on the chair while he sat on the floor with everyone else. At the opening of a market complex in Surat, he found a lavish seat prepared for himself and a simple one for Thakorji — he gave the grand one to God and took the plain one, sitting down only after the devotees had properly adorned Thakorji's seat. "God is almighty; we are his servants," he instructs. "Make sure his seat is higher than mine."

### One foot off the ground
In London in 1988, the youths begged Swamishri to enter their assembly seated in the palkhi they had lovingly prepared. He refused: "It's not right for me to sit in a palkhi in front of Thakorji. Place Harikrishna Maharaj in the palkhi — I will walk." After long persuasion he relented, on strict terms: he would sit only with Harikrishna Maharaj in his lap, and the youths had to promise never to lift the palkhi more than one foot off the ground. He looked down, uneasy, the whole way in, and breathed easier only once it was set down — after which he was seen praying to Thakorji for forgiveness for what he believed was a grave mistake.

### Surgery at three o'clock
In 1998 at Lenox Hill Hospital in New York, the doctors urged an immediate emergency bypass. Swamishri insisted on waiting until 3:00 p.m., and the surgeons reluctantly agreed; he entered the operation theatre with Thakorji seated beside him. Two weeks later, the sadhus asked why he had delayed a life-threatening operation. "If we went in at three o'clock, we would be able to serve Thakorji thal and let him rest for a while before waking him," Swamishri explained. "We could work according to his schedule and not inconvenience him." Even being wheeled out of an earlier cataract surgery, his first words had been, "Have you put Thakorji to rest? It is already time."

### The day Thakorji fell
At a cold-storage inauguration near Bochasan in 1990, the sadhu carrying Harikrishna Maharaj crashed through a weak plank in the wooden floor, and the murti fell from his hands. Swamishri rushed over, picked Thakorji up and massaged the murti's whole body with his fingers, tears welling as he apologized. In the car he kept murmuring, "Bhagwan Swaminarayan… my Lord… forgive me… I am sorry." At the assembly afterwards he managed about three minutes of speech before his voice choked with emotion and he pushed the microphone away. Years earlier, when Thakorji had fallen from the top tier of a caravan in America through no one's fault, Swamishri had performed 25 dandvats in apology.

### "Piling stones"
Bhanuben of Nadiad told her husband that mandirs were "like piling stones on top of each other — your Swami should build hospitals and community centres instead." That very evening, Swamishri visited their home and, discussing the new mandir in Vaso, said, "It's a shame some people think mandirs are just lifeless stone structures. They are a source of spirituality and values. There is a time in everyone's life when they need solace and comfort — mandirs provide that peace. That is why we build them." Bhanuben, listening from the next room, felt devotion surge in her heart. She never called them stones again.

### The atheist architect
Satish Gujral, the renowned architect and brother of a former Indian Prime Minister, had never believed in God, never visited mandirs, never bowed to murtis. In November 2005 he toured Swaminarayan Akshardham in New Delhi — and then found he couldn't resist meeting its creator. "I am an atheist, but this monument has the power to make an atheist believe in God!" he confessed. "Given the limitations in India, this project should have taken about fifty years — Swamishri completed it in five. Monuments like these cannot be explained with logic." That same month, another visitor's staunchly atheist son fell at Swamishri's feet: "I have never bowed to anyone before, but I felt a spiritual current jolt my body when he touched me."

### "Will you give us strength through the mala?"
In London in 1997, a teenager at a farewell assembly told Swamishri that all the youths would turn their new malas every night and think of him. "Will you give us strength through the mala? You will, right? Promise?" Swamishri answered with something far bigger: "Bhagwan Swaminarayan has already promised all of his devotees that. Never say 'Who will take care of us?' God, the Gunatit Sadhu and all the akshar muktas are always with us — while we eat, sleep, talk, everything. With that understanding you will be comfortable wherever you go. Faith is the source of strength in life — the faith that God is always with us."

## Anahamkrutihi · Humility · અનહંકૃતિઃ

### "I am just a servant"
No one has ever managed to make Swamishri take credit for Swaminarayan Akshardham — not in meetings, private conversations or public assemblies. Whenever he is praised for the monument, he lowers his head: "No, no, I haven't done anything. This is all because of Shastriji Maharaj and Yogiji Maharaj's blessings, Bhagwan Swaminarayan's grace, and the efforts of all the sadhus and devotees. Praise them. I am just a servant." A police superintendent once told him he had been longing to meet "the creator of Akshardham." Swamishri replied, "God is the creator. He inspires all of us."

### The ocean and the pot of water
A journalist asked Swamishri whether he ever tired of the constant travel and the weight of a growing worldwide organization. "It is God's work — I don't believe that I am doing anything," Swamishri said, and offered an image: "You only feel the burden on your head if you try to carry a pot of water. If you swim in the ocean, thousands of gallons are above your head and you don't feel a thing. Always pray to God saying, 'You are doing everything through me.'" Then he shared the secret of the whole Sanstha: "None of us believe that we have done anything. God is the all-doer. That principle lets everyone share the credit — so there is no jealousy or hatred."

### "We might have to get your ears checked"
After yet another dignitary left Swamishri's room gushing about Akshardham, a sadhu teased him: "Everyone who comes here can't stop complimenting you, and you don't even seem to notice. We might have to get your ears checked!" "No, no, I can hear everything," Swamishri said. "If you aren't going deaf, why don't you ever respond or react?" The answer came simply: "I hear that they are praising my guru Yogiji Maharaj and Bhagwan Swaminarayan. It is their greatness, not mine."

### "I cannot surpass my gurus"
In 1993, a respected Jain muni told Swamishri, "You have surpassed all of the previous gurus." Swamishri looked down, as if he himself had insulted or offended his gurus. "There is no way I can surpass them. I cannot surpass my gurus. If they hadn't laid the foundations, I wouldn't have been able to do anything. They have done it all." The muni realized that in Pramukh Swami Maharaj's eyes, guru bhakti — devotion to his gurus — was his greatest accomplishment.

### Ice cream or Indian sweets?
A youth coordinator in London wanted to buy the youths a treat and asked Swamishri, "What should I get for everyone — ice cream or Indian sweets?" "Whatever you want to offer to Harikrishna Maharaj," Swamishri replied. The coordinator pressed on: "You are our God. God works through you." Swamishri stopped him: "Bhagwan Swaminarayan is God. I am his servant. I am a devotee." "But doesn't God reside in you?" "God resides in one who becomes his servant!"

### "Man can never be God"
Impressed by Akshardham, the chairman of a large Indian corporation told Swamishri, "This is beyond my imagination. You have to be God!" "No. I am his servant," Swamishri answered. "Man can never be God." In Dallas, an American youth asked the same question from a Christian frame: "We believe God was present through Jesus. If that is true, who are you — God or human?" "There is only one God," Swamishri said. "A sadhu helps spread God's message. No one can become God. There is only one God, and I am his servant."

### "No one except God can succeed"
In 2006, a district judge relayed a towering compliment: at a conference of chief justices, President Abdul Kalam had said that Pramukh Swami Maharaj can succeed where technology and contemporary science have failed, with Akshardham as the proof. Swamishri deflected it instantly: "No one except God can succeed. God is the source of energy for everyone and everything. He enables us to raise our hand and put it down. If he wants us to succeed, we will — and if we do not, there must be a reason. God knows what's best for us."

### "That thought never goes away"
In Houston, a devotee asked Swamishri how he stayed humble while sadhus praised him to his face. "They are praising us because of God," Swamishri said. "If we don't remember that, we would become arrogant." The devotee pressed: "How often do you think that?" Swamishri's reply was quiet and complete: "That thought never goes away."
